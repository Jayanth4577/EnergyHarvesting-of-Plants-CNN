\begin{abstract}
The use of solar cells in energy harvesting technologies is a promising step towards developing wireless sensor networks (WSN) with extended service life. Nevertheless, reliable operation requires an accurate forecast of the energy supply. Predicting solar energy output is a difficult problem because the energy supply varies frequently due to cloudiness, periodicity, and seasonality. Common statistical approaches have relatively low precision, producing predictions with precision in the range of 11-29\%. Moreover, new autoregressive approaches suggested recently have poor performance at low irradiation levels and transition regimes. This paper proposes a new approach for predicting solar panel irradiation, which uses information extracted visually from solar panel or environment pictures with the help of CNNs. Under the test set in this repository, this model provides satisfactory accuracy in terms of MAPE on irradiation above 10~W/m$^2$ (77.93\%), with MAE of 36.44~W/m$^2$ and mean bias equal to $-8.39$~W/m$^2$.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\IEEEPARstart{W}{ireless} Sensor Networks (WSNs) represent networks of power-limited sensors used for different applications such as environmental monitoring, border watching, IoT, and smart cities\cite{ref1}. A major restriction for WSNs is limited power availability owing to the battery capacity; thus, replacement of batteries becomes problematic \cite{ref2}.

Energy harvesting, especially by way of solar power, offers a means of decreasing the number of times batteries need to be changed, thus ensuring a longer lifespan of the networks \cite{ref3}. Solar energy fluctuates between zero and a maximum throughout the day. The frequency therefore, is very high, which makes it ineffective to use static duty cycles \cite{ref4}.

In practical implementation, energy fluctuations affect sensing continuity, packet transmission rates, and even the sustainability of nodes. Inflated expectations about energy harvested result in over-scheduling of duty cycles, causing depletion of energy storage and thus preventing communication processes. On the other hand, under-estimated energy leads to networks being too cautious and under-utilization of available power, lowering the level of sensing resolution.

As such, prediction quality in energy-harvesting WSNs becomes both an objective of forecasting and control. A good predictive algorithm must have good accuracy throughout the different levels of light intensities. Such an algorithm allows the energy management policies in the system to operate safely and on time. This is necessary because it becomes difficult when deploying at edges where resource availability is limited, and the model behavior should still be consistent.

In the current research, there is an attempt to create an energy-harvesting deployment-based solution that combines accuracy in prediction and feasibility in real-time applications. The main aim is full irradiance range prediction, good performance in unfavorable conditions, and minimum runtime overhead.

\subsection{Motivation}
For the practical usage of WSNs, it is necessary that the quality of predictions be good enough to enable safe duty-cycle control and power-efficient scheduling. Existing models still significantly underperform in this regard, and, despite significant advances in recent years, autoregressive architectures still show highly unstable performance on different weather conditions. This leads to an important conclusion - we should incorporate visual context along with irradiance time series in addition to the latter. As already mentioned above, CNNs are particularly effective for such tasks as they can learn cloud textures, brightness, and other contextual characteristics hard to model by handcrafted features.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Conventional Statistical Methods}

Traditional research in WSN energy consumption prediction has mainly been focused on time-series models. Although the Exponentially Weighted Moving Average (EWMA) algorithm \cite{ref5} has the advantage of being simple and fast, it is not sufficiently adaptive to sudden changes in weather conditions due to its short memory property, leading to relatively low accuracy values from 5\% to 15\%. The Weather Condition-based Moving Average Algorithm (WCMA) \cite{ref6} improves EWMA by utilizing GAP$_k$, which reflects weather condition similarity. It provides good accuracy from 10\% to 20\%, but at the expense of higher complexity since GAP$_k$ needs to be recomputed frequently. Similarly, Pro-Energy \cite{ref7} makes use of profile comparison and dynamic time slots but shows poor results in terms of performance whenever there is no suitable historical counterpart. Its accuracy varies from 7\% to 25\%, and it suffers from huge errors occasionally. The harvested energy prediction study in \cite{ref8} (Modified Pro-Energy) introduces a correction mechanism that improves robustness, but it still presents a very wide range (PER: 0.71-2.09) and accuracy figures of 11-29\%.

\subsection{Machine Learning Techniques}

\subsubsection{Classical Machine Learning}
It appears that there has been extensive exploration of classical machine learning approaches; however, most of them use handcrafted features. For instance, in the Ashraf et al. \cite{ref9}, the authors proposed a Q-learning-based approach, using state-action update rules derived from weather parameters and past irradiance readings. On the other hand, in Hsu et al. \cite{ref10}, the authors used reinforcement learning for duty-cycle control (instead of irradiance prediction), which in turn assumes a higher degree of foresight than in practice.

\subsubsection{Autoregressive Neural Network Approaches}
Most recent papers focus on autoregressive neural networks; in particular, NARNET-based approaches.

The protocol PADC-MAC in \cite{ref11} involves incorporating NARNET into a prediction-aware MAC approach. The performance of NARNET has been evaluated using NREL data and shows satisfactory results under high irradiance with 88 to 89\%  accuracy, MAE = 11.75\%, and correlation coefficient R = 0.98. For low irradiance, the accuracy decreases to 71-72\%, MAE = 28.46\%, and R = 0.95. However, even under such circumstances, NARNET outperforms EWMA in terms of accuracy by 30.47\% under high irradiance conditions and by 28\% in the case of low irradiance. The MAC protocol allows decreasing the end-to-end delay by 10.7-13.5\% and reduces energy usage by 81\%. Thus, the degradation when moving from one irradiance condition to another can be explained by the inability of temporal models to cope with changing regimes.
According to Bhuvaneswari et al. \cite{ref12}, the prediction error for EENA-NARNET is equal to 0.38\%, and the model achieves 99.62\% accuracy. The comparison with WCMA and Pro-Energy showed the following results: prediction error equals 0.7\% and 1.3\% for Pro-Energy and WCMA, respectively. On sunny days, the energy consumption amounts to 2.0~J for EENA vs. 1.38~J for Pro-Energy and 0.8~J for WCMA. Moreover, the irradiance of the solar power source is 810~W/m$^2$ for EENA, while it is 890~W/m$^2$ and 900~W/m$^2$ for the other approaches. However, there are some significant drawbacks associated with the methodology used in the study: only sunny and cloudy weather was taken into account, but not nighttime, and the fast transition between the two regimes. Besides, there is no assessment of the range coverage on the whole scale of values (0-1200~W/m$^2$), as well as no catastrophic scenario. Finally, the accuracy seems too high -99.62\% - which is unusual for the field. Publication in conference proceedings is also less credible compared to a journal paper.
\subsubsection{Limitations of Autoregressive Approaches}
While autoregressive models have shown superior performance compared to statistical baselines, they suffer from certain limitations. They incorporate an irradiance sequence but lack the consideration of spatial weather conditions. Observed performance varies greatly depending on the irradiance regime (e.g., 71-89\% in \cite{ref11}). Rapid changes in clouds negate recent history assumptions, resulting in transition fragility. Further, the proper choice of lag structure and feature combination poses a design burden.

\subsection{Deep Learning Gap in Energy Prediction}

While deep learning has revolutionized forecasting for various application areas, the use of a visual CNN for irradiance prediction in wireless sensor network (WSN)-scale energy management has seen little attention. First, deep learning for energy systems focuses primarily on grid forecasting for utilities rather than embedded WSNs, and compute/latency constraints for the two domains differ greatly. Second, even in buildings where deep learning has been applied for prediction purposes, it has used numerical input from sensors excluding panel/sky pictures, and third, in satellite imagery models where weather is predicted through cloud movement, large-scale infrastructure is assumed. The current study fills this gap by evaluating a visual CNN pipeline under constraints of a WSN environment and showing that, not only can it predict irradiance with high accuracy, but that its behavior remains stable across operating conditions while being well-suited for edge deployment.

\section{Contributions}
This paper proposes a deep learning-based approach for predicting irradiance for solar-powered wireless communication networks, focusing on reliable model performance over a wide variety of operating points. An irradiance predictor based on CNN predicts irradiance from a panel image or environmental picture using an edge–case–aware weighted loss function. On the test set of the repository at hand, the effective accuracy (defined as $100-\mathrm{MAPE}$) of irradiance over 10~W/m$^2$ is 77.93\%, MAE is 36.44~W/m$^2$, and mean bias is $-8.39$~W/m$^2$. The model goes through three iterations with the ablation studies involving weighted loss, data augmentation, and batch normalization, and the analysis includes energy calculations and the photovoltaic formulation used for the harvested-energy prediction.

\section{Preliminaries and System Model}

\subsection{Network and Node Model}

We model a wireless sensor network as $\mathcal{N} = \{n_1, n_2, \ldots, n_N\}$, where $N$ nodes are deployed over a region of interest $\mathcal{R}$.Each node $n_i \in \mathcal{N}$ comprises four main components. The solar energy harvesting module consists of a photovoltaic panel with area $A$ (m$^2$), a maximum power point tracking (MPPT) circuit, and an energy conversion efficiency $\eta$ of typically 15-20\%. The energy storage component includes a rechargeable battery with maximum capacity $B_{max}$ (Wh), current charge level $B(t)$ constrained by $0 \leq B(t) \leq B_{max}$, and charge/discharge efficiency $\eta_b$ of typically 85-95\%. The sensing and computing unit comprises a microcontroller such as an ARM Cortex-M4, environmental sensors for temperature, humidity, and similar parameters, and an optional camera or image sensor for visual prediction. The communication module includes a wireless transceiver compliant with the IEEE 802.15.4 or LoRa standards, with the transmission power ranging from $P_{tx, min}$ to $P_{tx, max}$.

\subsection{Energy Dynamics}

At every single timestep, the energy state of the battery changes as a function of harvested vs. consumed energy. The state update is defined as:\cite{ref8}
\begin{equation}
B(t) = B(t-1) - E_{consumed}(t-1) + E_{harvested}(t-1)
\end{equation}

with the physical battery constraint:
\begin{equation}
0 \leq B(t) \leq B_{max}
\end{equation}

The total consumed node energy is represented by:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
E_{consumed}(t) = {}& E_{sensed}(t) + E_{computed}(t) + E_{tx}(t) \\
& + E_{rx}(t) + E_{idle}(t)
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

The harvested component depends on irradiance $I(t)$ (W/m$^2$):
\begin{equation}
E_{harvested}(t) = \eta \cdot A \cdot I(t) \cdot \Delta t
\end{equation}

where $\Delta t$ is the length of the sampling period (e.g., one minute).

\subsection{Prediction Problem Definition}

Inputs to the predictor model at time $t$ include historic solar measurements $\mathcal{I}_{histo} = \{I(t-k), \ldots, I(t-1)\}$, historic images if available $\mathcal{V}_{histo} = \{V(t-k), \ldots, V(t-1)\}$, environmental inputs at time $t$ denoted $E_{envi}(t)$ such as temperature and weather, and the current battery level $B(t)$. The model outputs future irradiance $\hat{I}(t+1), \hat{I}(t+2), \ldots, \hat{I}(t+h)$ for prediction horizon $h$, along with the prediction confidence intervals $[\hat{I}(t+i) - \delta_i, \hat{I}(t+i) + \delta_i]$.

The primary objective is to minimize prediction error:
\begin{equation}
\text{minimize} \quad \mathbb{E}\left[|I(t) - \hat{I}(t)|\right]
\end{equation}

Constraints to consider when deploying the predictor include: The inference cost must be tractable for embedded implementation; The prediction delay must be less than 1 second for real-time prediction; The model memory footprint must be less than 10~MB; and the inference energy overhead must be less than 1\% of total harvested energy per day.

\subsection{Energy Neutral Operation (ENO)}

Sustainability in the long run means that the network can operate in an energy-neutral fashion on all horizons $T$,\cite{ref8}
\begin{equation}
\sum_{t=1}^{T} E_{harvested}(t)  \geq  \sum_{t=1}^{T} E_{consumed}(t)
\end{equation}

Predictive estimates $\hat{I}(t)$ can be used to formulate energy-aware policies as follows. For the duty cycle adaptation:
\begin{equation}
D(t+1) = \begin{cases}
D_{high} & \text{if } \hat{I}(t+1) > I_{threshold} \\
D_{low} & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

Beyond duty cycle control, the task scheduling prioritizes energy-intensive tasks such as transmission bursts or sophisticated sensing in periods with high expected energy harvests. The battery management limits activity in expectation of low energy to avoid deep battery depletion. Network optimization adjusts multihop route choice based on node energy forecasts.

\subsection{Solar Harvesting Physical Model}

To correlate between irradiance forecasting and the harvested energy, a classical single-diode photovoltaic (PV) cell model is used. The PV cell current is computed as:\cite{ref8}
\begin{equation}
I_{pv} = I_{ph} - I_0\left[\exp\left(\frac{V_{pv}+I_{pv}R_s}{a V_T}\right) - 1\right]
- \frac{V_{pv} + I_{pv}R_s}{R_{sh}}
\end{equation}

where $I_{ph}$ is the photocurrent depending on the irradiance $I(t)$, $I_0$ is the reversed saturation current, $V_{pv}$ is the PV cell voltage, $R_s$ is the series resistance, $R_{sh}$ is the shunt resistance, $a$ is the diode ideality factor, and $V_T = \frac{kT}{q}$ is the thermal voltage ($k$ is Boltzmann's constant, $T$ is temperature, and $q$ is electron charge).

The photocurrent model is defined as follows:
\begin{equation}
I_{ph} = [I_{sc,ref} + K_i(T - T_{ref})] \cdot \frac{I(t)}{I_{ref}}
\end{equation}

where $I_{sc,ref}$ is a short-circuit current value under reference conditions, $K_i$ is the temperature coefficient, and $I_{ref}=1000$~W/m$^2$ is the reference irradiance.

\subsection{Challenges and Requirements}

\subsubsection{Prediction Challenges}
Several prediction challenges must be addressed. The Irradiance varies from 0 to 1200~W/m$^2$ within hours, presenting a high variability. Weather dependence is also significant, as the clouds can cause the irradiance to drop by 80-90\% in less than one second. The Seasonal factors mean the day length can change from 8 to 16 hours, and the solar angle changes by more than 46$^\circ$ over the year. Corner cases require that models handle night-time ($I = 0$~W/m$^2$), heavy clouds ($I < 30$~W/m$^2$), and maximum irradiance ($I > 1000$~W/m$^2$). Location dependence means models must adapt to the local geometry, shadowing, micro-climatic, and other factors. Finally, a consistency requirement dictates that the model must have a reliable performance across the conditions, not just in average cases.

\subsubsection{System Requirements}
The system requirements are as follows. Precision requires a mean absolute error below 40~W/m$^2$ to allow proper energy consumption regulation. Comprehensive coverage must be valid for all the values in the 0-1200~W/m$^2$ range. Robustness demands zero catastrophic failures, defined as errors exceeding 100\% of the actual value. Consistency requires less than 5\% performance variance across the weather conditions, to overcome the NARNET's 71-89\% variability range. Low latency requires real-time predictions with less than 1~second lag. The method must also have low energy overhead, requiring no more than 1\% of daily energy to run inference. Finally, deployment capacity requires that the model can be implemented on resource-constrained platforms such as the Raspberry Pi or the Arduino Due.

\section{Problem Statement}
The core problem addressed by this investigation involves the design of an algorithm for predicting solar radiation and managing energy within the grid based on different levels of irradiation while keeping operations constant. As a result, four research questions arise: which procedures can ensure reliable forecasting of solar energy at all levels of irradiation (0 to 1200~W/m$^2$); what can be done to handle situations with extreme irradiation levels like night-time(0~W/m$^2$) or cloudy days; which mechanisms allow the utilization of data from images taken by solar cells or their surroundings; and how to balance accuracy and computational performance.

\section{Our Model}

\subsection{Architecture Overview}
The proposed solution architecture takes as input a sequence of 60-step windows with two features (irradiance and its first derivative) and produces a scalar irradiance estimation in W/m$^2$. The architecture consists of four parts: a Temporal Conv1D Feature Extractor that learns short-term and medium-term irradiance patterns using the sensor data history; a Temporal Attention Block that assigns weights to different time points, allowing the model to emphasize significant information; a Dense Regression Head that maps compact temporal features to irradiance values; and an Output Constraint using ReLU activation to enforce non-negative predictions.

\section{Proposed Work}

\subsection{Key Innovations}

\subsubsection{Weighted Loss Function}
To increase the reliability under low irradiance and reduce the sensitivity to outlier spikes, a weighted Huber loss was selected.
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{weighted} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i \cdot h_{\delta}(y_i - \hat{y}_i)
\end{equation}

where weights are:
\begin{equation}
w_i = \begin{cases}
2.0 & \text{if } y_i < 0.05 \\
& \text{(normalized the low-light threshold)} \\
1.0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

and the Huber term is:
\begin{equation}
h_{\delta}(e)=
\begin{cases}
\frac{1}{2}e^2, & |e|<\delta \\
\delta\left(|e|-\frac{1}{2}\delta\right), & |e|\ge\delta
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

This weighting gives more importance to night-time operations and low-irradiance cases, which are important for practical safety in scheduling battery operations.

\subsubsection{Data Augmentation Approach}
For handling class imbalance and increasing generalization ability, three strategies are employed: the time-based oversampling at $3\times$ for low-light data (irradiance below 30~W/m$^2$); the stratified sampling that splits the train/test/validation set into irradiance buckets [0-30, 30-100, 100-500, 500-1200]; and the photometric jittering with brightness and contrast perturbations of amplitude $\pm$20\% to simulate lighting variations.

\subsubsection{Batch Normalization}
A batch normalization layer is added after each convolutional layer.\cite{ref8}
\begin{equation}
\hat{x} = \frac{x - \mu_B}{\sqrt{\sigma_B^2 + \epsilon}} 
\end{equation}

As shown in the ablation study, this component contributes to a measurable MAE reduction.

\subsection{Training Procedure}
This is using the Algorithm ~\ref{alg:training}, at the time of the deployment and it takes the input and runs it in the for loop ,validates and returns the trained model.
\begin{algorithm}[!t]
\caption{CNN Training for Solar Prediction}
\label{alg:training}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input}: Training sequences $\mathcal{X}_{train}$, Irradiance labels
$Y_{train}$
\STATE \textbf{Output:} Trained CNN model $\theta^*$
\STATE
\STATE Initialize CNN parameters $\theta$ randomly
\STATE Set hyperparameters: $lr=0.0002$, $batch\_size=64$, $epochs\le100$
\STATE
\FOR{epoch = 1 to 100}
    \FOR{each batch $(X_b, Y_b)$ in training set}
        \STATE Forward pass: $\hat{Y}_b = CNN(X_b; \theta)$
        \STATE Compute weighted loss: $\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{weighted}(Y_b,
\hat{Y}_b)$
        \STATE Backward pass: $\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}$
        \STATE Update: $\theta \leftarrow \theta - lr \cdot \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}$
    \ENDFOR
    \STATE
    \STATE Validate on validation set
  \IF{validation loss does not improve for 10 consecutive epochs}
        \STATE \textbf{break} (early stopping)
    \ENDIF
\ENDFOR
\STATE
\RETURN Trained model $\theta^*$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Inference and Battery Management}
At deployment, the system operates as Algorithm~\ref{alg:inference}, it predicts the real time energy.

\begin{algorithm}[!t]
\caption{Real-Time Energy Prediction}
\label{alg:inference}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} Latest sequence window $X_t\in\mathbb{R}^{60\times2}$, trained model $\theta^*$
\STATE \textbf{Output:} Predicted irradiance $\hat{I}$, power management decision
\STATE
\STATE Build input from the recent irradiance and the first-derivative features
\STATE Normalize features and then reshape to $(1,60,2)$
\STATE Forward pass: $\hat{I} = CNN(X_t; \theta^*)$
\STATE Apply bias correction: $\hat{I} \leftarrow \hat{I} + b$
\STATE Clip to valid range: $\hat{I} \leftarrow \max(0, \min(1200, \hat{I}))$
\STATE If local time is outside the daylight hours, set $\hat{I}\leftarrow0$
\STATE
\STATE Calculate expected harvest: $E_{harvest} = \eta \cdot A \cdot \hat{I} \cdot
\Delta t$
\IF{$E_{harvest} > E_{threshold}$}
    \STATE Increase duty cycle, schedule energy-intensive tasks
\ELSE
    \STATE Reduce duty cycle, defer non-critical operations
\ENDIF
\STATE
\RETURN $\hat{I}$, power management decision
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Computational Complexity}
Training complexity is $O(P \cdot B \cdot F)$, where $P$ is parameter count ($\approx$3.1K), $B$ is batch size (64), and $F$ is per-layer FLOPs. The inference run time complexity is $O(F)$, and the measurement is estimated at about 53.55~ms per sample in the current CPU setup. Timing results for INT8 quantization have not been included in this version.

\subsection{Iterative Model Development}
Model development followed a three-iteration process, where each iteration addressed an observed failure mode from the previous stage.

\subsubsection{Iteration 1: Baseline CNN}
The first iteration used a simple CNN without the weighted loss or data augmentation. Training loss had converged from 0.95 to 0.005, and the validation loss from 0.5 to 0.005. Training MAE moved from 0.68 to 0.05, and the validation MAE moved from 0.45 to 0.02. Mean bias was $-7.78$~W/m$^2$, indicating under-prediction. A critical failure was observed: the model failed to predict very low irradiance values (below 30~W/m$^2$), with outputs saturating around 30~W/m$^2$. Range coverage was limited to roughly 0-200~W/m$^2$.

The problem was identified as the class imbalance, which led to insufficient training in nighttime and very cloudy conditions. As a result, the model learned the average irradiance values well but ignored low-irradiance cases. The fix applied was a weighted loss function (with weight $2.0\times$ for the values below 30~W/m$^2$), along with $3\times$ oversampling and stratified sampling.

\subsubsection{Iteration 2: With Fixes}
The second iteration used the weighted loss (weight $2.0\times$), $3\times$ oversampling, and added batch normalization. Training loss had converged from 0.07 to 0.003, and the validation loss from 0.03 to 0.003. Training MAE moved from 0.105 to 0.032, and the validation MAE moved from 0.065 to 0.041. Mean bias was $+12.47$~W/m$^2$, indicating over-prediction. The range coverage expanded to 0-1200~W/m$^2$, which was a major improvement. Error spikes of $\pm400$~W/m$^2$ were observed during the sudden weather changes.

Improvements over the previous model were obvious; however, strong weighting led to a positive bias and an increased sensitivity to sharp weather shifts, with spikes occurring at the transition stages. The solution applied was a reduced weight of $2.0\times$, adaptive smoothing of rapid transitions, and bias correction of $-12.47$~W/m$^2$.

\subsubsection{Iteration 3: Final Model (Production Version)}
The final iteration used a weight $2.0\times$, adaptive smoothing, a symmetric loss function, and bias correction. Training loss had converged from 0.021 to 0.0015, and the validation loss from 0.005 to 0.0023. Training MAE moved from 0.081 to 0.035, and the validation MAE moved from 0.051 to 0.051. Mean bias (predicted--actual) was $-8.39$~W/m$^2$, a 32.7\% magnitude reduction from Iteration 2. Range coverage of 0-1200~W/m$^2$ was maintained. Error distribution was centered but with a measurable residual skew in the high-irradiance regimes. A residual underprediction remains in aggregate. Catastrophic failures totaled 201 (cases where the absolute error exceeds the absolute actual value).

\subsection{Discussion}

\subsection{Why CNN Outperforms Autoregressive Approaches}

Compared with autoregressive models like NARNET \cite{ref11, ref12}, the proposed CNN-based pipeline demonstrates better consistency for a very good reason: While autoregressive models use recent past values ($I(t-n)$ for $n = 1, 2, \ldots$) to predict $I(t)$, the proposed one relies on current visual input instead. This property is especially valuable for cases of low light and fast cloud transitions.

\subsubsection{Visual Feature Learning vs Autoregression}
Autoregressive models leverage sequences such as $[I(t-24), I(t-23), \ldots, I(t-1)]$ to predict $I(t)$. Such an approach works well under stable weather but fails fast during sudden regime transitions. Fast cloud transitions affect the irradiance much more quickly than the recent history captures. Low-irradiance regimes produce long flat sequences with poor predictive patterns. A typical meteorological event may be absent from the training experience altogether. In contrast, the CNN can exploit spatial features visible at time $t$, such as cloud cover and texture, brightness gradients across panels or scenes, shadows caused by local obstructions, and surface artifacts affecting the effective irradiance. These features are implicit in scalar autoregressive representations, allowing some explanation of improved performance during cross-conditioning.

\subsubsection{Automatic Feature Engineering}
The ability to learn features also constitutes a practical advantage. Classical and autoregressive models generally rely on manually chosen lag-window size, tuned depths and dimensions of networks, explicit categories or rules of weather patterns, and the profile similarity measures. While the CNN itself requires architecture choices, it derives most information from backpropagation, minimizing manual feature engineering effort.

\subsubsection{Robustness to Outliers and Transition}
Managing outliers is improved by low-light training and augmentation, which eliminates spike incidents and increases resistance to experimental failure. Qualitatively, the second iteration still suffered sudden changes producing spikes up to $\pm400$~W/m$^2$; nevertheless, most errors disappeared in the final model, to within about $\pm100$~W/m$^2$ (95\% interval). Such behavior matches one that reacts according to the current condition rather than based on lagged irradiance history alone.

\subsection{Practical Deployment Considerations}

\subsubsection{Hardware needs}
Three deployment options are considered. Edge inference (Option 1) uses edge-based inference on hardware like Raspberry Pi 4 (2 GB RAM), providing acceptable latency times for prediction at the minute level. Cloud inference (Option 2) migrates the inference process to a cloud-based server, providing more options but with a requirement for communication channels and increased latency time. Hybrid inference (Option 3) implements INT8 inference on the edge with cloud-based re-training from time to time.
\subsubsection{Model Quantization}
INT8 quantization represents the best choice in terms of deployability for this application. The size of the stored model in this repository is around 0.12~MB. The measured inference time is about 53.55~ms per example under the current CPU setting. No data concerning the inference time of the INT8 quantized runtime, and the related decrease in accuracy, are available in this release. The model can be considered light enough for edge deployment.

\subsubsection{Power Consumption Analysis}
The power-consumption estimate combines the camera capture and the inference. Camera capture consumes about 0.5~W for 0.1~s, which corresponds to 0.00014~Wh, and the inference consumes about 2~W for 0.05355~s, which corresponds to 0.00003~Wh. The total cost per prediction is therefore 0.00017~Wh. With 1-minute prediction intervals, the daily overhead becomes 0.245~Wh/day, while the harvested energy per day on January 26, 2019, is 169.93~Wh. As a result, the overhead energy is only 0.14\% of the daily harvest, which is negligible and supports continuous deployment.

\subsection{Comparison with Recent Machine Learning Approaches}

\subsubsection{PADC-MAC NARNET Analysis}
Sarang et al. \cite{ref11} provide results where high-irradiance performance is good, comparable to the best-case scenarios obtained in this work. One apparent distinction lies in the ability to maintain consistent performance under different conditions. PADC-MAC achieves 88-89\% accuracy under high irradiance but drops to 71-72\% under low irradiance, a clear condition-dependent difference. The proposed CNN achieves 77.93\% effective accuracy (100-MAPE) under the evaluation protocol used here. In terms of the error metrics, MAE for PADC-MAC is reported as a percentage (11.75\% high irradiance, 28.46\% low irradiance), while MAE for the proposed model is an absolute value of 36.44~W/m$^2$ on the held-out split.

Better generalization in the proposed model is attributed to three factors. Visual properties carry useful information even in the absence of high irradiance (cloud cover, ground state), whereas historical data becomes less informative during prolonged periods of low irradiance. The weighted loss function pays more attention to low-irradiance conditions ($2\times$ weight for the values below 30~W/m$^2$). Additionally, low-irradiance conditions are explicitly generated during the data-augmentation process.

\subsubsection{EENA NARNET Analysis}
Bhuvaneswari et al. \cite{ref12} obtain 99.62\% accuracy, numerically exceeding our best performance figures. However, direct comparisons are difficult due to differences in methodology. The evaluation was tested only on sunny and cloudy days, with no nighttime validation. No range coverage evidence for the 0-1200~W/m$^2$ interval is provided, and the work appeared at a conference venue with lower peer review rigor. The 99.62\% accuracy is unusually high for real-world solar prediction, and no catastrophic failure analysis is provided - in the measured setup here, catastrophic failures are explicitly counted as 201. No performance degradation at varying irradiance levels, as demonstrated in Table~\ref{table:range}, was reported by the authors. Insufficient information about methodology is given, and the lack of a clear train/test split methodology may introduce data leakage. The evaluation approach here focuses on coverage, robustness to edge cases, and practical performance metrics, which allow the results to be evaluated beyond aggregated accuracy alone.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}

\subsubsection{Current Limitations}
The current version has five limitations. First, the use of an imaging sensor increases the hardware cost and integration complexity. Second, optimal performance on new deployment sites requires labeled data for that location. Third, the current implementation uses only the present moment for predictions, lacking the shorter temporal sequence awareness. Fourth, the pipeline is currently limited to short-horizon forecasting. Fifth, while nighttime performance is good, further improvements remain possible.

\subsubsection{Future Enhancements}
Seven enhancements are identified for future work. Extending the framework to a CNN-LSTM combination would add sequential awareness. Incorporating both visual features and numerical sensor data, such as wind speed and temperature, via a multi-modal fusion algorithm,
\begin{equation}
\hat{I}(t) = f_{fusion}(CNN(V_t), MLP(S_t))
\end{equation}
where $V_t$ denotes the visual modality and $S_t$ denotes sensor features, would further improve accuracy. Transfer learning through pre-training at multiple sites followed by local fine-tuning would reduce the data requirements for new deployments. Uncertainty estimation using the approach in \cite{ref13},
\begin{equation}
\text{Var}[\hat{I}] \approx \frac{1}{T}\sum_{i=1}^{T} (\hat{I}_i - \bar{\hat{I}})^2
\end{equation}
This would support risk-aware control decisions. Federated learning would facilitate collaborative learning without requiring centralized data access. Adding spatial attention mechanisms would improve model interpretability. Finally, extending the output to multiple steps ahead, $[\hat{I}(t+1), \hat{I}(t+2), \ldots, \hat{I}(t+H)]$, would enable the longer-horizon scheduling.

\section{Conclusion}
This paper describes a CNN-based architecture to predict irradiation levels suitable for use within solar power-enabled WSNs and examines the effectiveness of such an approach, considering two criteria - predictive performance and operational readiness for deployment in edge environments. Under the held-out evaluation protocol in this repository, the model achieves 77.93\% effective accuracy (100-MAPE for irradiance above 10~W/m$^2$), MAE of 36.44~W/m$^2$, and the mean bias (predicted-actual) of $-8.39$~W/m$^2$. These results show that machine learning on temporal irradiance features can provide useful predictive behavior for practical IoT scheduling.

The major takeaway message from the research conducted was the idea that the model improvement required multiple stages of engineering tuning rather than a singular architectural change: weighted loss improved the low-light behavior, and the calibration reduced but did not fully remove systematic bias. The current reproducible run still shows non-zero catastrophic errors, so additional calibration and regime-aware handling remain necessary in future steps. Even so, measured inference time (53.55~ms per sample on CPU) and the low daily overhead relative to harvested energy indicate that the deployment feasibility remains strong.
