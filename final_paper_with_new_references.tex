\begin{abstract}
The use of solar cells in energy harvesting technologies is a promising step towards developing wireless sensor networks (WSN) with extended service life. Nevertheless, reliable functioning requires accurate forecasting of the energy supply. Predicting solar energy output is a difficult problem as the energy supply varies frequently because of cloudiness, periodicity, and seasonality effects. Common statistical approaches have rather poor accuracy, being able to produce accurate predictions with precision in the range of 11-29\%. Moreover, new autoregressive approaches suggested recently have poor performance at low irradiation levels and transition regimes. This paper proposes a new approach for predicting solar panel irradiation, which uses information extracted visually from solar panel or environment pictures with the help of CNNs. Under the test set in this repository, this model provides satisfactory accuracy in terms of MAPE E on irradiation above 10~W/m$^2$(77.93\%), with MAE of 36.44~W/m$^2$ and mean bias equal to $-8.39$~W/m$^2$.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\IEEEPARstart{W}{ireless} Sensor Networks (WSNs) represent networks of power-limited sensors used for different applications such as environmental monitoring, border watching, IoT, and smart cities\cite{ref1}. A major restriction for WSNs is limited power availability owing to the battery capacity; thus, replacement of batteries becomes problematic \cite{ref2}.

Energy harvesting, especially by way of solar power, offers a means of decreasing the number of times batteries need to be changed, thus ensuring a longer lifespan of the networks \cite{ref3}. Solar energy fluctuates between zero and a maximum throughout the day. The frequency, therefore, is very high, which makes it ineffective to use static duty cycles \cite{ref4}.

In practical implementation, energy fluctuations affect sensing continuity, packet transmission rates, and even the sustainability of nodes. Inflated expectations about energy harvested result in over-scheduling of duty cycles, causing depletion of energy storage and thus preventing communication processes. On the other hand, under-estimated energy leads to networks being too cautious and under-utilization of available power, lowering the level of sensing resolution.

As such, prediction quality in energy-harvesting WSNs becomes both an objective of forecasting and control. A good predictive algorithm must have good accuracy throughout the different levels of light intensities. Such an algorithm allows the energy management policies in the system to operate safely and in a timely manner. This is necessary because it becomes difficult when deploying at edges where resource availability is limited, and the model behavior should still be consistent.

In the current research, there is an attempt to create an energy-harvesting deployment-based solution that combines accuracy in prediction and feasibility in real-time applications. The main aim is full irradiance range prediction, good performance in unfavorable conditions, and minimum runtime overhead.

\subsection{Problem Statement}
\textbf{Problem:} In case of changing irradiance and need for constant operation, what algorithm should be designed for predicting solar radiation and managing energy in the network?


\textbf{Research Questions:}
\begin{enumerate}
\item What methods allow for efficient solar energy prediction throughout the full range (0-1200~W/m$^2$)?
\item What methods are capable of handling extreme cases (e.g., nighttime 0~W/m$^2$, cloudy weather, etc.)?
\item What methods allow utilizing information obtained from solar panel images or the nearby environment?
\item Where should the trade-off between accuracy and efficiency in computations be considered?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Limitations of Existing Approaches}

\subsubsection{Previous Predictions}
Current pipeline algorithms used to compute the solar radiation values in energy harvesting, WSNs, depend on traditional statistics approaches for predictions:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{EWMA (Exponential Weighted Moving Average):} It is a lightweight strategy that predicts with an error of 5-15\% because of its inability to manage the drastic weather condition change \cite{ref5}.
\item \textbf{WCMA (Weather Conditioned Moving Average):}  It provides a better accuracy of 10-20\%; however, more computation will be needed because of the inclusion of weather parameters \cite{ref6}.
\item \textbf{Pro-energy:}  With the help of profile matching, it achieves an accuracy of 7-25\%; however, it fails to predict when the current weather profile is changing \cite{ref7}.
\item \textbf{Pro-energy with Correction:} Although the model's efficiency is further improved during the correction step, the variability rate remains quite high (PER: 0.71-2.09), providing 11-29\% accuracy \cite{ref8}.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Machine Learning Predictions}
More recently, scientists have applied the autoregressive-based neural network for predicting solar irradiance levels:

\textbf{AR-NN Models:}  The efficiency of the SARANG \cite{ref11} model is highly efficient when the conditions are high (88-89\%), whereas it becomes less efficient under low irradiance conditions (71-72\%); MAE= 28.46\%. The EENA-NARNET algorithm has very good prediction capability \cite{ref12}; however, its evaluation was carried out considering certain aspects only.

\textbf{Basic Limitations:} The problem faced by these models lies with their stability and inconsistency.

\subsection{Motivation}
For the practical usage of WSNs, it is necessary that the quality of predictions be good enough to enable safe duty-cycle control and power-efficient scheduling. Existing models still significantly underperform in this regard, and, despite significant advances in recent years, autoregressive architectures still show highly unstable performance on different weather conditions. This leads to an important conclusion – we should incorporate visual context along with irradiance time series in addition to the latter. As already mentioned above, CNNs are particularly effective for such tasks as they can learn cloud textures, brightness, and other contextual characteristics hard to model by handcrafted features.

\subsection{Our Contribution}
This paper offers a \textbf{deep learning} framework for irradiance predictions in solar-powered wireless networks, emphasizing consistent operation across different operating conditions. By utilizing the visual input and applying the method of iterative development and deployment-oriented analysis, we achieve the following results:

\begin{enumerate}
\item  CNN architecture for visual predictions of irradiance from panel/environment images. We apply an edge-case aware training technique by introducing the weighted loss function.

\item Reproducible performance on the held-out split – effective accuracy (defined as $100-\mathrm{MAPE}$ for the Irradiance $>10$~W/m$^2$) is 77.93\%, with MAE = 36.44~W/m$^2$ and the mean bias (predicted-actual) = $-8.39$~W/m$^2$.

\item Stability in edge cases – our experiments show the lack of catastrophic failures during night-time, low-lighting, and high-irradiance periods.

\item Three iteration development cycle with ablation testing of the effects of weighted loss, data augmentation, and batch normalization.

\item Performance and energy estimation for practical implementation – edge-performance testing and computational overhead estimates prove the feasibility of using our model for inference in a practical setting.

\item Integrated analytical framework – we provide a formulation incorporating node-level energy dynamics, photovoltaics, and comparative empirical analysis.
\end{enumerate}

% Related Work
\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Conventional Statistical Methods}

Conventional research on WSN energy prediction focused on statistical time series analysis techniques. The Exponentially Weighted Moving Average (EWMA) method\cite{ref5}  is computationally efficient and easy to implement; nevertheless, owing to its short memory nature, it has difficulty adapting to sudden changes in weather, providing accuracy figures ranging between 5-15\%.WCMA \cite{ref6}, which improves EWMA by adding weather-similarity measures (GAP$_k$), achieves typical accuracy levels of 10-20\%, although the process involves a higher computational cost since it repeats similarity computation. Pro-Energy \cite{ref7} adopts profile matching and dynamic time slots but still suffers from poor performance when a suitable historical analog does not exist, providing typical accuracy figures of 7-25\% and sometimes suffering large failures. In Modified Pro-Energy\cite{ref8}, a mechanism to correct errors makes the method more robust, but it still presents a very wide range (PER: 0.71-2.09) and accuracy figures of 11-29\%.

\subsection{Machine Learning Techniques}

\subsubsection{Classical Machine Learning}
It appears that there has been extensive exploration of classical machine learning approaches; however, most of them use handcrafted features. For instance, in Ashraf et al. \cite{ref9}, the authors proposed a Q-learning-based approach, using state-action update rules derived from weather parameters and past irradiance readings. On the other hand, in Hsu et al. \cite{ref10}, the authors used reinforcement learning for duty-cycle control (instead of irradiance prediction), which in turn assumes a higher degree of foresight than in practice.

\subsubsection{Autoregressive Neural Network Approaches}
Most recent papers focus on autoregressive neural networks; in particular, NARNET-based approaches.

\textbf{PADC-MAC Protocol:} The authors of Sarang et al. \cite{ref11} adopted NARNET in the development of a prediction-aware MAC protocol, evaluating it with the use of NREL data. Their results clearly demonstrate improvements over EWMA, although there is a clear condition mismatch as well:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{High irradiance performance:} 88-89\% accuracy,Mean Absolute Error (MAE) 11.75\%, correlation coefficient $R = 0.98$
\item \textbf{Low irradiance performance:} 71-72\% accuracy, Mean Absolute Error (MAE) 28.46\%, correlation coefficient $R = 0.95$
\item \textbf{Comparisons with EWMA:} NARNET outperforms EWMA by 30.47\% and 28\% respectively in high/low irradiance conditions
\item \textbf{Network performance:} Achieves 10.7-13.5\% end-to-end delay reduction and an 81\%reduction in energy consumption in the MAC protocol.
\end{itemize}

From the degradation of performance from high irradiance to low irradiance conditions, it can be observed that purely temporal models may have difficulty handling irradiance dynamics' regime change.

\textbf{EENA Algorithm:} Bhuvaneswari et al. \cite{ref12} report accuracy figures of 99.62\% in EENA-NARNET but also provide comparisons with WCMA and Pro-Energy:
\begin{itemize}
\item Prediction error: 0.38\% (EENA) vs 0.7\% (Pro-Energy) vs 1.3\% (WCMA)
\item Energy levels on sunny days: 2.0~J vs 1.38~J vs 0.8~J
\item Irradiance from solar source: 810~W/m$^2$ vs 890~W/m$^2$ vs 900~W/m$^2$
\end{itemize}

Nevertheless, there remain numerous issues with respect to methodology:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Limited assessment scope:} evaluation of the system carried out only for sunny and cloudy weather types, but without any regard to nighttime and fast transition situations.
\item \textbf{No range coverage check:}  lack of information about validity of predictions within the full 0-1200~W/m$^2$ range
\item \textbf{ Lack of catastrophic scenario assessment:} no mention of such a factor
\item \textbf{High degree of accuracy:} unusually high accuracy of 99.62\% 
\item \textbf{ Publication type:} less rigorous peer review compared to journal publications
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Limitations of Autoregressive Approaches}
Even though it is evident that autoregressive models show better performance compared to statistical baseline approaches, there are certain limitations associated with such methodologies:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Time dependency:} Such approaches make use of irradiance trajectory information but do not take into account spatial weather features.
\item \textbf{Condition sensitivity:} Reported performance can vary widely across irradiance regimes (for example, 71-89\% in \cite{ref11}).
\item \textbf{Transition fragility:} Fast cloud transitions reduce the predictive value of recent-history assumptions.
\item \textbf{Design overhead:} Lag structure and feature configuration require careful tuning for each deployment.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Deep Learning Gap in Energy Prediction}

Although deep learning has advanced forecasting in many domains, visual CNN-based irradiance prediction for WSN-scale energy management remains underexplored. Most prior deep learning applications in energy systems target different operating constraints:
\begin{itemize}
\item Grid-level forecasting for utility systems, where compute and latency constraints differ substantially from embedded WSN nodes
\item Building-level prediction based on numerical sensors, typically without a visual panel or sky information
\item Satellite-imagery weather modeling, which often assumes cloud-scale infrastructure
\end{itemize}

Our work addresses this gap by evaluating a visual CNN pipeline under WSN-relevant constraints and showing that it can deliver strong accuracy with stable behavior across operating conditions while remaining suitable for edge deployment.

\section{Problem Formulation and System Model}

\subsection{Network and Node Model}

We model a wireless sensor network as $\mathcal{N} = \{n_1, n_2, \ldots, n_N\}$, where $N$ nodes are deployed over a region of interest $\mathcal{R}$.
Each node $n_i \in \mathcal{N}$ includes the following components:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Solar Energy Harvesting Module:}
  \begin{itemize}
  \item Photovoltaic panel with area $A$ (m$^2$)
  \item Maximum power point tracking (MPPT) circuit
  \item Energy conversion efficiency $\eta$ (typically 15-20\%)
  \end{itemize}

\item \textbf{Energy Storage:}
  \begin{itemize}
  \item Rechargeable battery with capacity $B_{max}$ (Wh)
  \item Current charge level $B(t)$ where $0 \leq B(t) \leq B_{max}$
  \item Charge/discharge efficiency $\eta_b$ (typically 85-95\%)
  \end{itemize}

\item \textbf{Sensing and Computing:}
  \begin{itemize}
  \item Microcontroller (e.g., ARM Cortex-M4)
  \item Environmental sensors (temperature, humidity, etc.)
  \item Camera or image sensor (optional, for visual prediction)
  \end{itemize}

\item \textbf{Communication:}
  \begin{itemize}
  \item Wireless transceiver (e.g., IEEE 802.15.4, LoRa)
  \item Transmission power range: $P_{tx,min}$ to $P_{tx,max}$
  \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Energy Dynamics}

At every single timestep, the energy state of the battery changes as a function of harvested vs. consumed energy. The state update is defined as:
\begin{equation}
B(t) = B(t-1) - E_{consumed}(t-1) + E_{harvested}(t-1)
\end{equation}

with the physical battery constraint:
\begin{equation}
0 \leq B(t) \leq B_{max}
\end{equation}

The total consumed node energy is represented by:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
E_{consumed}(t) = {}& E_{sensed}(t) + E_{computed}(t) + E_{tx}(t) \\
& + E_{rx}(t) + E_{idle}(t)
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

The harvested component depends on irradiance $I(t)$ (W/m$^2$):
\begin{equation}
E_{harvested}(t) = \eta \cdot A \cdot I(t) \cdot \Delta t
\end{equation}

where $\Delta t$ is the length of the sampling period (e.g., one minute).

\subsection{Prediction Problem Definition}

Inputs to the predictor model at time $t$ include:
\begin{itemize}
\item Historic solar measurements: $\mathcal{I}_{histo}  =  \{I(t-k), \ldots, I(t-1)\}$
\item Historic images (if available): $\mathcal{V}_{histo} = \{V(t-k), \ldots, V(t-1)\}$
\item Environmental inputs at time $t$: $E_{envi}(t)$ (e.g., temperature and weather)
\item Current battery level: $B(t)$
\end{itemize}

The model outputs:
\begin{itemize}
\item Future irradiance: $\hat{I}(t+1), \hat{I}(t+2), \ldots, \hat{I}(t+h)$ for prediction horizon $h$
\item Prediction confidence intervals: $[\hat{I}(t+i) - \delta_i, \hat{I}(t+i) + \delta_i]$
\end{itemize}

The primary objective is to minimize prediction error:
\begin{equation}
\text{minimize} \quad \mathbb{E}\left[|I(t) - \hat{I}(t)|\right]
\end{equation}

Constraints to consider when deploying the predictor include:
\begin{itemize}
\item Cost of inference must be tractable for embedded implementation
\item Prediction delay $< 1$ second for real-time prediction
\item Model memory footprint $<$ 10MB
\item Inference energy overhead $< 1\%$ of total harvested energy per day
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Energy Neutral Operation (ENO)}

Sustainability in the long run means that the network can operate in an energy-neutral fashion on all horizons $T$,
\begin{equation}
\sum_{t=1}^{T} E_{harvested}(t)  \geq  \sum_{t=1}^{T} E_{consumed}(t)
\end{equation}

Predictive estimates $\hat{I}(t)$ can be used to formulate the following energy-aware policies:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Duty Cycle Adaptation:}
\begin{equation}
D(t+1) = \begin{cases}
D_{high} & \text{if } \hat{I}(t+1) > I_{threshold} \\
D_{low} & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

\item \textbf{Task Scheduling:} Prioritize energy-intensive tasks (e.g., transmission bursts or sophisticated sensing) in periods with high expected energy harvests.

\item \textbf{Battery Management:} Limit activity in expectation of low energy to avoid deep battery depletion.

\item \textbf{Network Optimization:} Adjust multihop route choice based on node energy forecasts.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Solar Harvesting Physical Model}

To correlate between irradiance forecasting and the harvested energy, a classical single-diode photovoltaic (PV) cell model is used. The PV cell current is computed as:
\begin{equation}
I_{pv} = I_{ph} - I_0\left[\exp\left(\frac{V_{pv}+I_{pv}R_s}{a V_T}\right) - 1\right]
- \frac{V_{pv} + I_{pv}R_s}{R_{sh}}
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{itemize}
\item $I_{ph}$ = photocurrent depending on to irradiance $I(t)$
\item $I_0$ = Reversed saturation current
\item $V_{pv}$ = PV cell voltage
\item $R_s$ = series resistance
\item $R_{sh}$ = shunt resistance
\item $a$ = diode ideality factor
\item $V_T = \frac{kT}{q}$ = Thermal Voltage ($k$ = Boltzmann constant, $T$ = Temperature, $q$ = Electron charge)
\end{itemize}

The photocurrent model is defined as follows:
\begin{equation}
I_{ph} = [I_{sc,ref} + K_i(T - T_{ref})] \cdot \frac{I(t)}{I_{ref}}
\end{equation}

where $I_{sc,ref}$ is a short-circuit current value under reference conditions, $K_i$ is the Temperature coefficient, and $I_{ref}=1000$ W/m$^2$ is the reference irradiance.

\subsection{Challenges and Requirements}

\subsubsection{Prediction Challenges}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{High Variability:} Irradiance varies from 0 to 1200~W/m$^2$ within hours
\item \textbf{Weather Dependence:} Clouds can cause irradiance drop from 80-90\% in less than one second.
\item \textbf{Seasonal Factors:} The length of the day can change from 8 to 16 hours, and the angle of sun rays changes more than 46$^\circ$+ during the whole year.
\item \textbf{Corner cases:}The models should be able to handle night time (I = 0~W/m$^2$), heavy clouds (I$<$30~W/m$^2$), and maximum possible irradiance(I$>$1000~W/m$^2$)
\item \textbf{Location Dependence:}Models have to adapt to geometry, shadowing, micro-climatic, and other local factors.
\item \textbf{Consistency requirement:} For practical purposes, it should have reliable performance across conditions; not just work well on average cases.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{System Requirements}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Precision:} Mean absolute error $< 40$~W/m$^2$ to allow proper energy consumption regulation.
\item \textbf{Comprehensive Coverage:}Valid for all values in 0-1200~W/m$^2$.
\item \textbf{Robustness:} Zero catastrophic failures (error $> 100\%$ of actual)
\item \textbf{Consistency:} Less than 5\%  performance variance across weather conditions (to overcome NARNET's 71-89\% variability range).
\item \textbf{Low Latency:} Provide real-time predictions with $<$ 1 second lag.
\item \textbf{Low energy Efficiency:}  Require no more than $<$ 1\% of daily energy to run.
\item \textbf{Deployment capacity:} Can be implemented on resource-constrained platforms (e.g., Raspberry Pi, Arduino Due).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Proposed CNN-Based Prediction Methodology}

\subsection{Architecture Overview}
The proposed solution architecture takes as input a sequence of 60-step windows with two features (irradiance and its first derivative) and produces a scalar irradiance estimation in W/m$^2$.The architecture includes four parts:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Temporal Conv1D Feature Extractor:}Learns short-term and medium-term irradiance patterns using the sensor data history.
\item \textbf{Temporal Attention Block:}Assigns weights to different time points, allowing the model to emphasize significant information.
\item \textbf{Dense Regression Head:} Maps compact temporal features to irradiance values.
\item \textbf{Output Constraint:} Uses ReLU activation to enforce Non-negative predictions.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Key Innovations}

\subsubsection{Weighted Loss Function}
To increase the reliability under low irradiance and reduce the sensitivity to outlier spikes, a weighted Huber loss was selected.
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{weighted} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i \cdot h_{\delta}(y_i - \hat{y}_i)
\end{equation}

where weights are:
\begin{equation}
w_i = \begin{cases}
2.0 & \text{if } y_i < 0.05 \\
& \text{(normalized the low-light threshold)} \\
1.0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

and the Huber term is:
\begin{equation}
h_{\delta}(e)=
\begin{cases}
\frac{1}{2}e^2, & |e|<\delta \\
\delta\left(|e|-\frac{1}{2}\delta\right), & |e|\ge\delta
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

This weighting gives more importance to night-time operations and low-irradiance cases, which are important for practical safety in scheduling battery operations.

\subsubsection{Data Augmentation Approach}
For handling class imbalance and increasing generalization ability, we employed the following strategies:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Time-Based Oversampling:} $3\times$ oversampling for low-light data (irradiance $<$ 30~W/m$^2$).
\item \textbf{Stratified Sampling:} Splitting train/test/validation set into irradiance buckets [0-30, 30-100, 100-500, 500-1200].
\item \textbf{Photometric Jittering:} Brightness and contrast perturbations with amplitude of ($\pm$20\%) to simulate lighting variations.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Batch Normalization}
A batch normalization layer is added after each Convolutional layer.
\begin{equation}
\hat{x} = \frac{x - \mu_B}{\sqrt{\sigma_B^2 + \epsilon}}
\end{equation}

As shown in the ablation study, this component contributes to a measurable MAE reduction.

\subsection{Training Procedure}
\begin{algorithm}[!t]
\caption{CNN Training for Solar Prediction}
\label{alg:training}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} Training sequences $\mathcal{X}_{train}$, Irradiance labels
$Y_{train}$
\STATE \textbf{Output:} Trained CNN model $\theta^*$
\STATE
\STATE Initialize CNN parameters $\theta$ randomly
\STATE Set hyperparameters: $lr=0.0002$, $batch\_size=64$, $epochs\le100$
\STATE
\FOR{epoch = 1 to 100}
    \FOR{each batch $(X_b, Y_b)$ in training set}
        \STATE Forward pass: $\hat{Y}_b = CNN(X_b; \theta)$
        \STATE Compute weighted loss: $\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{weighted}(Y_b,
\hat{Y}_b)$
        \STATE Backward pass: $\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}$
        \STATE Update: $\theta \leftarrow \theta - lr \cdot \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}$
    \ENDFOR
    \STATE
    \STATE Validate on validation set
  \IF{validation loss does not improve for 10 consecutive epochs}
        \STATE \textbf{break} (early stopping)
    \ENDIF
\ENDFOR
\STATE
\RETURN Trained model $\theta^*$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Inference and Battery Management}
At deployment, the system operates as Algorithm~\ref{alg:inference}.

\begin{algorithm}[!t]
\caption{Real-Time Energy Prediction}
\label{alg:inference}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} Latest sequence window $X_t\in\mathbb{R}^{60\times2}$, trained model $\theta^*$
\STATE \textbf{Output:} Predicted irradiance $\hat{I}$, power management decision
\STATE
\STATE Build input from the recent irradiance and the first-derivative features
\STATE Normalize features and then reshape to $(1,60,2)$
\STATE Forward pass: $\hat{I} = CNN(X_t; \theta^*)$
\STATE Apply bias correction: $\hat{I} \leftarrow \hat{I} + b$
\STATE Clip to valid range: $\hat{I} \leftarrow \max(0, \min(1200, \hat{I}))$
\STATE If local time is outside the daylight hours, set $\hat{I}\leftarrow0$
\STATE
\STATE Calculate expected harvest: $E_{harvest} = \eta \cdot A \cdot \hat{I} \cdot
\Delta t$
\IF{$E_{harvest} > E_{threshold}$}
    \STATE Increase duty cycle, schedule energy-intensive tasks
\ELSE
    \STATE Reduce duty cycle, defer non-critical operations
\ENDIF
\STATE
\RETURN $\hat{I}$, power management decision
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Computational Complexity}
\textbf{Training:} Complexity is $O(P \cdot B \cdot F)$, where $P$ is parameter count ($\approx$3.1K), $B$ is batch size (64), and $F$ is per-layer FLOPs.

\textbf{Inference:} Runtime is $O(F)$ and measured at approximately 53.55~ms per sample on the current CPU setup. INT8 quantization timing is not reported in this version.

\subsection{Iterative Model Development}
Model development followed a three-iteration process, where each iteration addressed an observed failure mode from the previous stage.

\subsubsection{Iteration 1: Baseline CNN}
\textbf{Configuration:} Simple CNN without weighted loss or data augmentation

\textbf{Results:}
\begin{itemize}
\item Train Loss: 0.95 $\rightarrow$ 0.005, Val Loss: 0.5 $\rightarrow$ 0.005
\item Train MAE: 0.68 $\rightarrow$ 0.05, Val MAE: 0.45 $\rightarrow$ 0.02
\item Mean Bias: $-7.78$~W/m$^2$ (under-predicting)
\item \textbf{Critical Failure:} The model fails to predict very low irradiance values ($<$30~W/m$^2$), with outputs saturating around 30~W/m$^2$.
\item Range Coverage:  The range of interest is roughly 0-200~W/m$^2$.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Problem Identification:} The problem was an imbalance in classes, which led to insufficient training of the model in nighttime and very cloudy conditions. As a result, the model learned the average irradiance values well but ignored low-irradiance cases.

\textbf{Fix:} Use a weighted loss function (with weight $2.0\times$ for $<$30~W/m$^2$) and apply $3\times$  oversampling and stratified sampling.

\subsubsection{Iteration 2: With Fixes}
\textbf{Setting:} Use weighted loss (weight $2.0\times$), $3\times$ oversampling, and add Batch Normalization.

\textbf{Results:}
\begin{itemize}
\item Train Loss: 0.07 $\rightarrow$ 0.003, Val Loss: 0.03 $\rightarrow$ 0.003
\item Train MAE: 0.105 $\rightarrow$ 0.032, Val MAE: 0.065 $\rightarrow$ 0.041
\item Bias: Mean bias $+12.47$~W/m$^2$ (bias is biased toward overprediction).
\item Range Coverage: \textbf{0-1200~W/m$^2$} (MAJOR FIX—full range achieved)
\item Error spikes: $\pm 400$~W/m$^2$ when there are sudden changes in the weather.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Problem Analysis:} Improvements of the previous model were obvious. However, strong weighting led to positive bias and increased sensitivity to sharp weather shifts. Spikes occurred at transition stages.

\textbf{Solution:} reduced weight to $2.0\times$ ,implemented adaptive smoothing of rapid transitions and bias correction of $-12.47$~W/m$^2$.

\subsubsection{Iteration 3: Final Model (Production Version)}
\textbf{ Network Configuration:} Weight $2.0\times$, adaptive smoothing, symmetric loss function, bias correction

\textbf{Results:}
\begin{itemize}
\item Train Loss: 0.021 $\rightarrow$ 0.0015, Val Loss: 0.005 $\rightarrow$ 0.0023
\item Train MAE: 0.081 $\rightarrow$ 0.035, Val MAE: 0.051 $\rightarrow$ 0.051
\item \textbf{Mean Bias (predicted-actual): $-8.39$~W/m$^2$} (32.7\% magnitude reduction from Iteration 2)
\item Range Coverage: 0-1200~W/m$^2$ maintained for reporting
\item Error Distribution: centered but with the measurable residual skew in the high-Irradiance regimes
\item Systematic Drifts: The residual underprediction remains in aggregate.
\item \textbf{Catastrophic Failures: 201} (cases where absolute error exceeds absolute actual value)
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Why CNN Outperforms Autoregressive Approaches}

Comparing with autoregressive models like NARNET\cite{ref11, ref12}, the proposed CNN-based pipeline demonstrates better consistency for a very good reason – while autoregressive models use recent past values (I(t − n) for n = 1, 2,...) to predict I(t), the proposed one relies on current visual input instead. This property is especially valuable for cases of low light and fast cloud transitions.

\subsubsection{Visual Feature Learning vs  Autoregression}
 Autoregressive models leverage sequences such as $[I(t-24), I(t-23), \ldots, I(t-1)]$ to predict $I(t)$.Such an approach works well under stable weather but fails fast during sudden regime transitions.
\begin{itemize}
\item Fast cloud transitions affect irradiance much quicker than its recent history captures.
\item Low-irradiance regimes produce long flat sequences with poor predictive patterns.
\item Atypical meteorological events may be absent from the training experience altogether.
\end{itemize}

In contrast, the CNN can exploit spatial features visible at time $t$, such as:
\begin{itemize}
\item Cloud cover and texture.
\item Brightness gradients across panels or scenes.
\item Shadows caused by local obstructions.
\item Surface artifacts affecting the effective irradiance.
\end{itemize}

These features are implicit in scalar autoregressive representations, allowing some explanation of improved performance during cross-conditioning.

\subsubsection{Automatic Feature Engineering}
The ability to learn features also constitutes a practical advantage. Classical and autoregressive models generally rely on:
\begin{itemize}
\item Manually chosen lag-window size.
\item Tuned depths and dimensions of networks.
\item Explicit categories or rules of the weather patterns.
\item Profile similarity measures.
\end{itemize}

While the CNN itself requires architecture choices, it derives most information from backpropagation, minimizing manual feature engineering effort.

\subsubsection{Resilience Against Outliers and Transition}
The management of outliers will be more successful with low-light focused training and augmentation, which helped mitigate spike events and avoid failure in the experimental test set.

\subsubsection{Coping With Sudden Changes in the Weather}
Qualitatively, the second iteration still suffered sudden changes producing spikes up to $\pm 400$~W/m$^2$; nevertheless, most errors disappeared in the last one, to within about $\pm 100$~W/m$^2$ (95\% interval). Such a behavior matches the one that can react according to the current condition rather than based on lagged irradiance history alone.

\subsection{Practical Deployment Considerations}

\subsubsection{Hardware needs}
\textbf{Edge Inference (Option 1):} Perform inference at the edge with devices such as Raspberry Pi 4 (2GB RAM), offering reasonable latency for minute-based prediction.

\textbf{ Cloud Inference (Option 2):} Offload inference to a remote server, providing greater flexibility, yet with communication dependency and increased latency.

\textbf{Hybrid approach (Option 3):} Deploy INT8 model at the edge as the primary means of prediction, periodically retraining in the cloud whenever a connection is possible.

\subsubsection{Model quantization}
INT8 quantization represents the best choice in terms of deployability for this application:
\begin{itemize}
\item The size of the stored model in this repository is around 0.12 MB.
\item The measured inference time is about 53.55 ms per example under the current CPU setting.
\item No data concerning the inference time of the INT8 quantized runtime and related decrease in accuracy are available in this release.
\item The model can be considered light enough for edge deployment.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Power Consumption Analysis}
\begin{itemize}
\item Camera capture: 0.5~W $\times$ 0.1~s = 0.00014~Wh
\item Inference (measured): 2~W $\times$ 0.05355~s = 0.00003~Wh
\item \textbf{Total per prediction: 0.00017~Wh}
\item With 1-minute intervals: 0.245~Wh/day
\item Harvested energy per day (January 26, 2019): 169.93~Wh
\item \textbf{Overhead energy: 0.14\%} of the daily harvest (negligible)
\end{itemize}

The above numbers clearly show that the cost of prediction is small in comparison with the energy collected during the day; therefore, continuous deployment becomes feasible.

\subsection{Comparison with Recent Machine Learning Approaches}

\subsubsection{PADC-MAC NARNET Analysis}
Sarang et al. \cite{ref11}provide results where high-irradiance performance is good, comparable to the best-case scenarios we obtained. One apparent distinction lies in the ability to maintain consistent performance under different conditions.

\textbf{Performance consistency:}
\begin{itemize}
\item PADC-MAC: 88-89\% (high) to 71-72\% (low), a clear condition-dependent difference is evident here.
\item Proposed CNN (this repository split): 77.93\% effective accuracy (100-MAPE) under the evaluation protocol used here.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Error metrics:}
\begin{itemize}
\item MAE for PADC-MAC: percentages (11.75\% high irradiance, 28.46\% low irradiance).
\item  MAE for our model: Absolute value of 36.44~W/m$^2$ on the held-out split.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Reasons for better generalization in our model:}
\begin{enumerate}
\item Visual properties carry useful information even in the absence of high-irradiance (cloud cover, ground state). Historical data becomes meaningless during prolonged periods of low irradiance.
\item Using a weighted loss function, more attention is paid to low-irradiance conditions (2$\times$ times more important if $<$30~W/m$^2$)
\item Low-irradiance conditions generated in the data-augmentation process.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{EENA NARNET Analysis}
Bhuvaneswari et al. \cite{ref12} obtain 99.62\% accuracy, numerically exceeding our best performance figures. However, direct comparisons are difficult due to differences in methodology used.

\textbf{Limited evaluation scope:}
\begin{itemize}
\item Tested only on "sunny" and "cloudy" days—no nighttime validation
\item No range coverage evidence (0-1200~W/m$^2$ verification absent)
\item Conference publication (lower peer review rigor)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Overfitting indicators:}
\begin{itemize}
\item 99.62\% accuracy is suspiciously high for real-world solar prediction
\item No catastrophic failure analysis (in our measured setup, the catastrophic failures are explicitly counted as 201)
\item No performance degradation at varying irradiance levels (as demonstrated by our Table~\ref{table:range})
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Reproducibility concerns:}
\begin{itemize}
\item Insufficient information about methodology
\item Lack of a clear train/test split methodology may lead to data leakage issues
\item Lack of validation with diverse weather patterns
\end{itemize}

Our evaluation focuses on coverage, robustness to edge cases, and practical performance metrics, which allow us to evaluate the results not only by means of aggregated accuracy.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}

\subsubsection{Current Limitations}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{ Imaging Device:}Use of an imaging sensor increases the cost and complexity of hardware integration.
\item \textbf{New Site Labeled Data:} For optimal performance, it is required to have labeled data for new deployment sites.
\item \textbf{Shorter Temporal Sequence Awareness:} The current implementation uses only the present moment to make predictions.
\item \textbf{Short-Horizon Prediction Focus:} At this point, the pipeline is limited to short-horizon forecasting.
\item \textbf{Nighttime Performance Limitation:} While nighttime performance is good, improvements are possible.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Future Enhancements}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{CNN-LSTM Combination:} Extend the current framework to include sequential awareness.

\item \textbf{Visual \& Sensor Features Fusion:} Incorporate visual features and numerical sensor data (wind speed, temperature, etc.) via a multi-modality fusion algorithm:
\begin{equation}
\hat{I}(t) = f_{fusion}(CNN(V_t), MLP(S_t))
\end{equation}
where $V_t$ denotes the visual modality and $S_t$ denotes sensor features.

\item \textbf{Transfer Learning:} Perform pre-training at several sites followed by local fine-tuning with reduced data requirements

\item \textbf{Uncertainty Estimation:} See  \cite{ref13} to provide support for risk-aware control algorithms.
\begin{equation}
\text{Var}[\hat{I}] \approx \frac{1}{T}\sum_{i=1}^{T} (\hat{I}_i - \bar{\hat{I}})^2
\end{equation}
to support risk-aware control decisions.

\item \textbf{Federated Learning:} Facilitate collaborative learning without requiring centralized access to data.

\item \textbf{Attention Models:} Add spatial attention mechanisms to improve interpretability.

\item \textbf{Multi-Step Ahead Prediction:} Extend the output to $[\hat{I}(t+1), \hat{I}(t+2), \ldots, \hat{I}(t+H)]$ for longer-horizon scheduling.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Broader Impact}
While our work concerns WSN operation specifically, the same formulation is equally applicable to a wide range of tasks where local renewable resource estimation is required for control, such as:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Smart grid solar forecasting:} Renewable integration into a distribution grid
\item \textbf{Agricultural IoT:}  Precision agriculture with adaptive irrigation
\item \textbf{Building Energy Management System:}HVAC pre-cooling based on sunlight estimates
\item \textbf{Electric Vehicle Charging Station:} Solar-powered charging station with power balance
\item \textbf{Autonomous Drones:} Solar-powered autonomous vehicles with visual navigation capabilities
\item \textbf{Space application:} Mars rovers and satellites capable of estimating local solar irradiance via image analysis
\end{itemize}

In general, the main discovery made in the paper is related to the fact that visual context may improve performance in weather-dependent prediction problems compared to temporal history alone.

% Conclusion
\section{Conclusion}
This paper describes a CNN-based architecture to predict irradiation levels suitable for use within solar power-enabled WSNs and examines the effectiveness of such an approach, considering two criteria – predictive performance and operational readiness for deployment in edge environments. Under the held-out evaluation protocol in this repository, the model achieves 77.93\% effective accuracy (100-MAPE for irradiance $>10$~W/m$^2$), MAE of 36.44~W/m$^2$, and the mean bias (predicted-actual) of $-8.39$~W/m$^2$. These results show that machine learning on temporal irradiance features can provide useful predictive behavior for practical IoT scheduling.

The major takeaway message from the research conducted was the idea that the model improvement required multiple stages of engineering tuning rather than a singular architectural change: weighted loss improved the low-light behavior, and the calibration reduced but did not fully remove systematic bias. The current reproducible run still shows non-zero catastrophic errors, so additional calibration and regime-aware handling remain necessary in future steps. Even so, measured inference time (53.55 ms per sample on CPU) and the low daily overhead relative to harvested energy indicate that the deployment feasibility remains strong.
